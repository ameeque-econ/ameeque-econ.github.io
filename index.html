Intellectual Foundations

For a long time, my world was bounded by the quiet hum of econometric software and the stark columns of census microdata. I spent my days tracking educational infrastructure rollouts in Latin America or parsing household datasets, treating complex human decisions as clean coefficients and identification strategies. Yet, staring at these matrices of numbers, I often felt a persistent silence. A parameter can prove *that* an intervention shifted a lifecycle outcome, but it remains mute on the *why*—the invisible web of historical inertia, unspoken social norms, and raw human struggle that actually dictates whether a public policy takes root or falls apart. I realized that behind every decimal point was a heartbeat, a history, and a silent narrative that numbers alone could never fully reconstruct.

To find those silent stories, I stepped away from the spreadsheets and looked toward a different kind of data. I began reading classics, moral philosophy, and existential novels, seeking a qualitative stress-test for the elegant but often rigid assumptions of rational choice and utility maximization. In the institutional struggles of Greek antiquity, the suffocating moral battlegrounds of Dostoevsky, and the post-colonial frictions of South Asian narratives, I found the true, messy, non-linear reality of human choice. These texts taught me that human agency does not operate in a vacuum; it is constantly colliding with collective memory, subconscious anxieties, and deep historical path dependency.

This collection is not a detour from my empirical work, but the very soil in which it is planted. When I investigate trust decay or the salience of post-partition identity, I am not just running Hierarchical Linear Models—I am analyzing the lived realities captured by Manto and Lieven. When I study how school construction reshapes female bargaining power, I am looking at structural shifts in agency that Tolstoy or Woolf spent lifetimes dissecting. By anchoring my quantitative frameworks in this literary landscape, I try to ensure that my development and policy research remains deeply human, intellectually resilient, and responsive to the rich, complex realities of the Global South.

Greek & Roman Antiquity

My encounter with classical antiquity was a turning point in how I define "state capacity." Staring at econometric models of public goods, I often wondered how civic trust first takes root in a society. Reading Plato's Republic and Aristotle’s Ethics forced me to stop viewing institutions as mere mathematical constraints on a spreadsheet, and start viewing them as deeply moral covenants. Grappling with the ancient struggles of Homer and Virgil taught me that the rules of economic exchange are built on historical mythologies and civic rituals. This ancient curriculum shifted my perspective: it made me realize that before we can measure a state's modern public policy, we must understand the foundational, qualitative moral frameworks that allow civic cooperation to exist in the first place.

The Russian Masters

I used to look at poverty and constraint through the sterile lens of budget boundaries and consumption frontiers. But when I read Dostoevsky and Tolstoy, my understanding of human welfare suffered a profound shock. Inside Crime and Punishment and Anna Karenina, I watched characters make choices that violated every assumption of perfect economic rationality—driven instead by guilt, spiritual longing, and systemic despair. These masters forced me to rebuild my behavioral framework from scratch. They taught me that human agency under pressure is volatile, non-linear, and deeply complex. This realization fundamentally changed how I evaluate social policy: I no longer design evaluations assuming people behave like clean economic agents, but rather as complex, struggling individuals navigating deep moral and cognitive dissonances.

  • The Brothers Karamazov — Fyodor Dostoevsky
  • Notes from Underground — Fyodor Dostoevsky

Perspectives on Pakistan

As an economist working on trust decay and national identity in post-partition Pakistan, this literature is not a passive reading list; it is a mirror to my own research. I spent months compiling GDELT and Google Trends data to map provincial identity salience, but it was Saadat Hasan Manto’s devastating partition stories and Mohsin Hamid's contemporary prose that gave those data points a voice. Reading these works made me feel the raw, human cost of borderland dynamics and post-colonial state-building. They taught me that national identity and social trust are not static parameters, but deeply volatile narratives constantly being negotiated. This qualitative depth directly guides my empirical research, transforming my statistical models from mere mathematical exercises into authentic historical process-tracing.

American Classics

My exploration of the American classics was an exercise in understanding the philosophical architecture of the modern market. Reading Ayn Rand's relentless individualism alongside Thoreau's radically quiet retreat at Walden Pond exposed me to the deep, structural tensions of capitalist societies. It forced me to interrogate my own assumptions about the balance between individual ambition and collective responsibility. These works evolved me from a technician who simply accepts market mechanics into a researcher who questions the very moral legitimacy of market incentives. I realized that public policies do not exist in an ideological vacuum; they are built on deeply contested philosophies of self-reliance and social welfare that shape how citizens interact with the state.

  • The Fountainhead — Ayn Rand
  • Thy Neighbor's Wife — Gay Talese
  • Walden — Henry David Thoreau
  • Moby-Dick — Herman Melville

The European Canon & Psychoanalysis

I have always found the rigid predictability of rational choice theory deeply unsatisfying. To bridge this gap, I turned to the dramatic depths of Dante, Milton, and the systematic excavations of Sigmund Freud. Navigating the circles of Dante's Inferno alongside Freud's mappings of the subconscious completely dismantled my belief in perfect rationality. They revealed to me that our choices are often guided by latent cognitive biases, deep-seated anxieties, and symbolic narratives we don't fully comprehend. This psychological curriculum fundamentally reshaped my methodological approach: it gave me the qualitative foundations to embrace behavioral economics, allowing me to view public policy not as a set of logical equations, but as an intervention in a complex landscape of human memory, bias, and motivation.

About Me

I am an M.Sc. graduate in Economics from HSE University (Moscow) and a Fulbright Alumnus. My research interests lie at the intersection of Development Economics and the Economics of Education, with a particular focus on using applied econometrics to evaluate public policy interventions. My analytical work is complemented by a deep personal interest in classical philosophy and literature, which shape my perspective on human behavior and social frameworks.

Previously, I worked as a Research Assistant at the Centre for Economic Research in Pakistan (CERP) and as a Research Intern at IFAT (Budapest). My methodological toolkit includes causal inference, the design and implementation of RCTs, and the analysis of large-scale household datasets using Stata and R.

Works in Progress

The Generational Shift: Dynamic Effects of Educational Infrastructure on Women’s Empowerment and Marriage Markets in Mexico and Brazil

Project in Progress

Evaluates the causal impact of staggered sub-national secondary school expansions on women's life-cycle outcomes in Mexico and Brazil. Utilizing IPUMS census microdata (1990–2020) matched with administrative school foundation records, this comparative study exploits geographic and temporal variation in access to secondary education. Methodologically, the study implements the Borusyak, Jaravel, and Spiess (2024) imputation estimator to address biases in traditional staggered Difference-in-Differences (TWFE) designs, testing labor market outcomes alongside marriage market signaling channels (assortative mating and spousal age gaps).

Does Asymmetric Cross-Border Cultural Exposure Weaken National Identity Salience and Generalized Social Trust?

Project in Progress

Investigates the impact of asymmetric cross-border cultural flows on state-centric national pride and generalized trust in post-partition Pakistan. This project implements a mixed-method design combining Hierarchical Linear Modeling (HLM) on World Values Survey microdata with province-level digital exposure indices derived from Google Trends and GDELT Project APIs, supplemented by qualitative historical process-tracing of state educational boundary maintenance and heritage-language buffering mechanisms.
